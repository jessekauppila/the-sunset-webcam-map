## Note on Ampersands and Word Counts

> **[Editor]** You asked: is **`&`** a word?
>
> Strictly speaking, no — the ampersand is a logogram (a typographic symbol, historically a ligature of the Latin "et") that *stands for* the word "and." It's not a word itself.
>
> In practice, for a grant application's word count:
> - Most graders treat it as equivalent to "and" — i.e., one word.
> - Some automated word-counters skip it entirely.
> - Either way it almost never saves you a word.
>
> For LACMA specifically, I'd recommend **spelling out "and"** in your formal answers. The `&` reads as casual / design-y / email-signature, and formal grant applications reward plain English. Save ampersands for your section headers or display text (where you might write "Art + Tech" rather than "Art and Tech" — stylistic, intentional).

## Project Analysis & Grant Fit Assessment

---

### What This Project Is

*Sunrise/Sunset* (working title) is a planetary-scale, real-time portrait of
sunrise and sunset — the daily terminator line sweeping across Earth —
constructed from hundreds of public webcams, a growing fleet of custom
edge-computing cameras, and an AI trained to recognize a beautiful sunset.
It operates across five interconnected layers:

**Live Visualization Layer:** A Next.js application renders an interactive
Mapbox map (2D) and deck.gl globe (3D) showing the day/night terminator ring.
It queries the Windy webcam API every minute to find webcams near the
terminator, then displays their live feeds as markers on the map and as
geographically-sorted mosaic grids — one for sunrise, one for sunset —
composed of dozens of simultaneous webcam views arranged by geographic
position.

**Custom Hardware Layer:** Small, bespoke edge-computing cameras built from
Raspberry Pi Zero 2 W units, designed to be deployed in specific geographic
locations to supplement commercial webcam APIs with higher-quality imagery
and finer control over streaming behavior. These cameras are intended both
as project infrastructure and as a potential Kickstarter object, allowing
the public to co-own a piece of the sensor network.

**Machine Perception Layer:** A full ML pipeline (PyTorch → ONNX) trains
computer vision models to evaluate "sunset quality" — essentially teaching a
machine to have aesthetic judgment about light and atmosphere. This is
augmented by a vision-LLM labeling system (Gemini/OpenAI/Claude) that rates
webcam images on quality, cloud formations, and other attributes, creating
a feedback loop between human ratings, AI ratings, and curated external
imagery (scraped from Flickr under CC licenses).

**Physical Installation Layer:** The current installation is a diptych —
two 27" portrait monitors driven by a Raspberry Pi 4B, one showing the
sunrise mosaic, one the sunset. The proposed expansion is a ring of inward-
facing monitors that places the viewer at the center of the Earth's
terminator, surrounded by the entire day–night boundary in real time.
Tile size in each mosaic scales by AI-determined quality, so the machine's
aesthetic judgment literally shapes the visual composition.

**Archival / Participatory Layer:** A public rating app ("a dating app for
sunsets") lets anyone online score archived snapshots, feeding back into
the ML training loop. The system captures and preserves "good" sunsets
(gated by AI score thresholds) to Firebase, building a growing archive of
the Earth's daily light show.

### How This Maps to LACMA's Evaluation Criteria

#### 1. "Is the project artist-led and does it have artistic merit?"

This is fundamentally an artwork about shared planetary experience mediated
through networked cameras and machine perception. The terminator — the
boundary between day and night — is a universal, continuous phenomenon that
every human on Earth experiences daily but never sees in aggregate. By
assembling low-fidelity webcam feeds into mosaics and extending them with
custom hardware, the project creates a kind of collective landscape painting
that paints itself — a portrait of the Earth's daily rhythm drawn from the
accidental infrastructure of surveillance and tourism cameras. The planned
ring installation directly echoes Janet Cardiff's *Forty-Part Motet*,
substituting sunsets for voices and the terminator for a choir.

#### 2. "Does the project explore emerging technology?"

Deeply. The project weaves together:

- **Computer vision / transfer learning** (ResNet18, MobileNetV3) to encode
  aesthetic judgment
- **Large language model vision APIs** (Gemini, GPT, Claude) as automated
  art critics / labelers
- **Real-time geospatial computation** (subsolar point calculations,
  terminator geometry)
- **Edge computing for art installations** (both Raspberry Pi kiosk systems
  and the custom Pi Zero 2 W cameras)
- **Custom hardware design** — PCBs, enclosures, remote-tuned streaming
  firmware
- The conceptual territory of **machines learning to see beauty** — training
  a model to distinguish a "good" sunset from a mediocre one is a
  fundamentally provocative act

#### 3. "Does the project suggest models, methods, and/or data that may be of interest to other artists and technologists?"

Yes — the entire codebase is on GitHub and produces shareable artifacts:

- **Open-source ML pipeline** for aesthetic image scoring
- **Open hardware** — designs and firmware for the Pi Zero edge cameras,
  potentially offered via Kickstarter as a participatory object
- **Webcam-to-mosaic rendering system** adaptable to other geospatial art

- **LLM-as-aesthete methodology** — using vision models to generate

- **Dataset / archive of sunrise/sunset images** with both human and machine
  labels

The project is inherently public-facing and demo-ready:
